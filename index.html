03 · Capstone Story & Reflection

Hospital Readmission Analysis — The Full Story

🔴 The Problem

Why do patients keep coming back?

Under the CMS Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program, hospitals face Medicare payment penalties when patients return within 30 days of discharge. Despite years of enforcement, wide performance gaps persist across states, clinical conditions, and hospital types. The core question was: what is actually driving those gaps — and is it something hospitals can realistically change?

🟡 The Data Journey

Challenges we worked through

The FY 2026 CMS HRRP dataset had 11,720 records across 2,833 hospitals in 51 states. A 31.4% suppression rate in discharge data, a right-skewed ERR distribution that ruled out standard parametric tests, and near-equal class balance all required careful methodological decisions. Choosing the right approaches — and verifying that CMS risk-adjustment was working — took more deliberate work than expected.

🔵 The Solution

Four methods, one consistent answer

We applied logistic regression (AUC 0.724) for interpretable Odds Ratios, a decision tree (AUC 0.737) for EHR-embeddable decision rules, K-Means clustering (k=4) to validate performance tiers, and Kruskal-Wallis tests to confirm geographic variation. Across all four, the same three predictors emerged — predicted readmission rate, clinical condition, and geography — accounting for 82.6% of all classification decisions.

🟢 Impact & Reflection

What I learned and what it means

The most surprising finding was the geographic effect — Massachusetts hospitals face 3.12× higher odds of High-Risk classification even after controlling for condition type. That is not a hospital quality problem; it is a structural one. This project taught me that analytics without context can mislead. My clinical background helped me ask the right questions, and I came away understanding that the gap between a good model and a useful recommendation is filled by domain knowledge.

06 · Publication

Peer-Reviewed Research

📄 JHSMR · Vol. 42, No. 5 · 2024 · Scopus Indexed
Echocardiography Evaluation of the Left Ventricular Mass Index (LVMI) and Geometrical Pattern in Pre-Eclampsia and Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension Patients
Thirumurugan E, Edwin Dias, Karthick R, Kalpana Devi H, Kaza Revanth Kumar, Kamaleshwari K, Karunya A, Manisha S, Madhumitha R
Objective: To evaluate the degree of hypertrophy and its geometrical patterns in hypertensive pregnant women, with or without proteinuria. Methods: Data from 88 pregnant women analyzed consecutively; clinical and echocardiographic data compared between normotensive and hypertensive groups. Results: Hypertensive women have significantly higher LV mass indexed by height than normotensive women (108.1±64.6 vs. 76.5±24.8 vs. 68.7±17.1 g/m, p=0.001). Conclusion: Women with pre-eclampsia and PIH show significant subclinical LV remodelling compared to normotensive pregnant women.
