03.5 / Capstone Story

The Capstone Story

🔴 The Problem

Why Do Patients Keep Coming Back?

Under the CMS Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program (HRRP), hospitals face Medicare payment penalties when patients are readmitted within 30 days of discharge. Despite years of penalties, performance gaps remain wide and persistent across states, conditions, and hospital types. The core question: what actually drives excess readmissions — and what can be done about it?

🟡 The Data Journey

Challenges We Faced

The FY 2026 CMS HRRP dataset contained 11,720 records across 2,833 hospitals in 51 states. Key challenges included a 31.4% suppression rate in discharge volume data, a right-skewed ERR distribution that ruled out standard parametric tests, and near-equal class balance that required careful model selection. Choosing the right methods — and validating that the CMS risk-adjustment was actually working — required working through each issue systematically.

🔵 The Solution

Four Methods, One Clear Answer

We applied four complementary analytical methods: logistic regression (AUC 0.724) for interpretable Odds Ratios, a decision tree classifier (AUC 0.737) for EHR-embeddable decision rules, K-Means clustering (k=4) to validate performance tiers, and Kruskal-Wallis non-parametric testing to formally confirm geographic variation. Across all four methods, the same three predictors emerged: predicted readmission rate, clinical condition, and geographic region — together accounting for 82.6% of all classification decisions.

🟢 The Impact

Five Actionable Recommendations

Findings led to five evidence-based CMS policy recommendations: condition-stratified HRRP intervention programs, an early-warning risk stratification tool deployable 12–18 months before penalty windows close, a Northeast geographic anomaly audit (Massachusetts OR 3.12×), targeted investment in the Challenged Performers segment (20.6% of hospitals), and minimum surgical volume thresholds for Hip & Knee HRRP inclusion. Each recommendation is directly traceable to model output.

Peer-Reviewed Research

📄 Journal of Health Science and Medical Research (JHSMR) · Vol. 42, No. 5 · 2024 · Scopus Indexed
Echocardiography Evaluation of the Left Ventricular Mass Index (LVMI) and Geometrical Pattern in Pre-Eclampsia and Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension Patients
Thirumurugan E, Edwin Dias, Karthick R, Kalpana Devi H, Kaza Revanth Kumar, Kamaleshwari K, Karunya A, Manisha S, Madhumitha R
Objective: To evaluate the degree of hypertrophy and its geometrical patterns in hypertensive pregnant women, with or without proteinuria. Methods: Data from 88 pregnant women analyzed consecutively. Both clinical and echocardiographic data between normotensive and hypertensive women were compared. Results: Hypertensive women, with and without proteinuria, have significantly higher LV mass indexed by height than normotensive women (108.1±64.6 vs. 76.5±24.8 vs. 68.7±17.1 g/m, p=0.001). Conclusion: Women with pre-eclampsia and pregnancy-induced hypertension show significant subclinical remodelling of the left ventricle compared to normotensive pregnant women.
